## What Is Agile?

**Agile** is a philosophy for getting complex work done in a way that is organized, transparent, and adaptable. It was formalized in 2001 by a group of software developers who were frustrated with the way large projects were managed — long planning phases, rigid timelines, and finished products that no longer matched what anyone actually needed by the time they were delivered.

The solution they proposed was to work in short, focused cycles. Plan a small amount of work. Do it. Review what you built. Adjust. Repeat.

**Scrum** is the most widely used framework for putting Agile values into practice. It defines specific roles, events, and artifacts that teams use to organize work into time-boxed cycles called sprints. When employers ask whether you have "Agile experience," they almost always mean Scrum.

Scrum spread quickly beyond software development. Today it is used by design teams, marketing departments, product companies, agencies, and startups across every industry. If you work in a professional creative or technical environment, you will almost certainly work this way.

## The Four Scrum Events

- flow-tabs

### Sprint Planning

At the start of every sprint, the team holds a **sprint planning** meeting. The goal is to answer one question: what can we commit to completing in this sprint?

The team reviews the backlog — the prioritized list of all work to be done — and selects the items they will tackle. They define a sprint goal: a short statement of what success looks like at the end of the sprint. Each selected item gets broken into concrete tasks.

Sprint planning turns a list of future work into a concrete, committed plan.

### Daily Standup

A **standup** (also called a daily scrum) is a short meeting — 15 minutes or less — held at the start of each working day. Every team member answers three questions:

1. What did I complete yesterday?
2. What will I work on today?
3. Is anything blocking my progress?

That's it. The standup is not a status report to a manager — it is a coordination tool for the team. It keeps everyone aware of where the work stands, surfaces problems early, and creates a daily rhythm of accountability.

The name comes from the original practice of holding the meeting standing up, so it stays short.

### Sprint Review

At the end of each sprint, the team holds a **sprint review** — sometimes called a demo. Work that was completed is shown to stakeholders. Incomplete work is noted and returned to the backlog. The team gets real feedback on what they built.

The sprint review creates a regular moment of honesty: did we do what we said we would do? If not, why not?

### Retrospective

After the sprint review, the team holds a **retrospective** — a short internal conversation about the *process* itself, not the work. Three questions:

1. What went well?
2. What did not go well?
3. What will we do differently next sprint?

The retrospective is how Scrum teams improve over time. It is where a good team becomes a great one.

## How This Classroom Maps to Scrum

This program runs one sprint per week, which compresses the full Scrum cycle into a classroom-friendly rhythm. Some events are combined — Monday is both sprint planning and a standup-style check-in rolled into one session.

| [light] | Scrum | This Classroom |
|---|---|---|
| Work cycle | Sprint (1–2 weeks) | Weekly credit module |
| Planning | Sprint planning | Monday work plan: what will I accomplish this week? |
| Progress check | Daily standup | Monday check-in: what did I complete last week? any blockers? |
| Deliverable deadline | Sprint review / demo | Friday credit submission |
| Process reflection | Retrospective | Thursday journal entry |
| Work to be done | Product backlog | Course sessions and assignments |

When you sit down on Monday morning and write your plan — what you completed last week, what you will accomplish this week, what is blocking you — you are doing sprint planning and a standup in one step. When you submit your completed work on Friday, you are completing a sprint. When you reflect in your journal on Thursday, you are running a retrospective.

You are not just learning design and code. You are practicing the professional workflow that employers expect.

## Why This Matters for Your Career

When you interview for a design or development role, you will almost certainly be asked about your experience working in Agile or Scrum environments. Most studios, agencies, and tech companies run this way. Being able to say — truthfully — that you have worked in weekly sprints, participated in planning sessions, met regular submission deadlines, and reflected on your own process puts you ahead of candidates who have only learned the technical skills.
