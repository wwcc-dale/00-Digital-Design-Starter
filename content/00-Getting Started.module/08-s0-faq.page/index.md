## Getting Started and Enrollment

- flow-accordion
###### Can I try the program before committing?
You can try out the program for one full week — up to five sessions — and withdraw with no questions asked. After five sessions, you can still request a withdrawal, but a program review is likely before a decision is made. If you're unsure whether this is the right fit, the best thing to do is show up and find out.

###### Which path should I start on — Graphic Design or Web Development?
Most students start with Graphic Design Essentials, and that is the recommended starting point. But if you are already drawn to building websites and writing code, starting with Web Design Fundamentals is equally valid. If you genuinely don't know yet, that is fine — talk to your instructor. Many students figure out their preference after their first few courses, and you can adjust your path without losing any of your completed work.

###### Do I need prior experience in design or coding?
No. Both starting certificates assume no prior experience. CS 110 (Intro to Computers & Applications) and the early design and web courses are built for students starting from the beginning. If you already have some experience, you will move through the early material quickly — which is fine.

###### What equipment do I need?
Lab computers are provided and have all required software installed. You do not need your own computer to participate. If you want to work outside of class, lab laptops may be available to take home — ask your instructor.

## Program Structure and Credentials

- flow-accordion
###### How do the stackable credentials work?
Every course you complete counts toward something — and shorter credentials stack directly into longer ones. Finishing the Graphic Design Essentials short certificate earns you a real credential, and every course in it counts toward the one-year Graphic Design Certificate and the AAS degree. Nothing is repeated. If your timeline changes and you have to leave the program earlier than planned, you leave with whatever you have earned.

###### What is the difference between a short certificate, a one-year certificate, and the AAS degree?
Short certificates (4 courses, roughly 6 months) are the entry point — a focused credential in either design or web fundamentals. One-year certificates (9 courses, roughly 12 months) go deeper and end with a capstone project. The AAS degree (the full two-year program) adds general education courses and gives you the most complete credential. All three are real, stackable, and useful on a résumé.

###### Can I switch paths after I have started?
Yes, with some planning. Talk to your instructor. Because the credentials stack, some of your completed courses will count toward multiple paths. Your instructor can map out what you have already completed and what the most efficient route forward is from where you are.

###### What is a capstone project?
Every one-year certificate and the AAS degree end with a capstone — a major project you build from start to finish on your own. Depending on your path, that is a complete launched website (BUS 288), a portfolio-ready design project (BUS 289), or a full-stack web application (CS 231). Capstones matter because they are real work you can show employers. A certificate with a strong capstone is proof you can do the job.

###### Are there general education courses?
General education courses — Technical Writing, Quantitative Problem Solving, and Communication in the Workplace — are required for the AAS degree but not for the certificates. They were recently dropped as certificate requirements. That said, they are genuinely valuable. Good writing, basic math, and professional communication skills regularly make the difference between getting hired and being passed over. If you have time, take them.

###### What software will I be using?
On the design side: Adobe Photoshop and Illustrator primarily, with exposure to Figma for interface and UX work. On the web development side: VS Code as a code editor, with HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and eventually back-end tools and databases. All software is available on lab computers.

## Pace, Workload, and Sessions

- flow-accordion

###### This is self-paced — can I take my time and go slowly?
Self-paced means you move through the material at your own computer on your own schedule — not that you can take as long as you want. Every student is expected to complete **one credit per week**. A credit is five sessions, each about three hours of focused work. If you consistently complete less than one credit per week, you will fall behind and may not finish your credential on time. Pace matters.

###### What does "one credit per week" mean in actual hours?
Roughly fifteen hours of focused work per week — five sessions at about three hours each. You can spread those sessions however you like. One per day is the most sustainable approach for most students. Three longer sessions is harder but doable. Two sessions is a pace problem waiting to happen.

###### What does a typical session look like?
Every session follows the same six-part structure: Reading → Video → Key Concepts Review → Exercise → Practice Quiz → Wrap-Up. A complete session is designed to fit within three hours. Some run faster, a few run longer. The exercise at the center of each session has three levels — Base (required), Intermediate, and Advanced — so you can push further if you have time.

###### What happens if I fall behind?
Talk to your instructor as soon as you notice — not at the end of the week. Falling behind compounds quickly: one late credit means the next credit starts late. Your instructor can help you figure out where you lost time and make a plan to recover. Asking for help before you fall behind is always better than asking after.

###### Can I take a laptop home to work outside of class?
Yes. Lab laptops may be available to take home. Work done outside of class counts the same as work done in the lab. If you use a laptop at home, save your files to your class folder on the shared drive — not just to the local laptop drive — so your work is accessible from any lab computer.

###### Does my laptop contain resources available in the classroom?
Yes. Your laptop has copies of most classroom resources stored on the F: drive in a folder called **course files**. This includes many of the video tutorials, images, book PDFs, website captures, and coursework used in class — so you can keep working even without an internet connection.

Your laptop also contains an offline copy of **freeCodeCamp**, which offers thousands of hours of hands-on coding and web development tutorials. It is there for extra practice whenever you have time and want to go further.

###### Photoshop is glitching out on my laptop — is there a fix?
Yes. Open Photoshop Preferences and uncheck **Use Graphics Processor**. The glitching should stop.

That said, Photoshop is honestly too demanding for the lab laptops to run well. The fix above helps, but for serious Photoshop work, class time on a lab computer is a better experience. Save the laptop for everything else.

###### Can I attend more than one session per day?
Ordinarily, no — one session per day is the standard. In rare cases, if attending both daily sessions would allow a student on a tight release timeline to finish a one-year certificate or the AAS degree, the instructor may request enrollment in both. This requires an instructor request and is not guaranteed. If this situation applies to you, talk to your instructor.

## Classroom Culture and Collaboration

- flow-accordion

###### Can I ask for help?
Yes — and you should. Asking for help when you are stuck is expected, not an imposition. Your instructor and any teaching assistants are there specifically to help you. If you have been stuck for more than a few minutes, raise your hand or catch your instructor's eye. Do not wait and hope the confusion resolves itself.

###### Can I help other students?
Yes, and it benefits you too. Explaining a concept to someone else forces you to organize your own understanding of it — students who help others regularly perform better on their own assessments. Help by explaining: walk the other student through the thinking, point to the instruction they need, ask questions that lead them to the answer. Do not do the work for them. Completing another student's assignment is not allowed.

###### Will everyone in the room be working on the same thing?
Almost certainly not. This is a non-cohort classroom, which means students enroll at different times and move at their own pace. On any given day, students around you may be in completely different courses. That is normal. Do not look around and compare — focus on your own work and your own pace.

###### What is Documentary Friday?
Once a week, the computers go off and the whole room watches a documentary together. The films are chosen to broaden your frame of reference — to show what professionals at the top of their fields accomplish, how creative work gets made, and where design and technology intersect with culture and human experience. Participation is mandatory. Log off your computer, put your phone away, and give the film your full attention. After the screening there is time for open discussion. You are encouraged to collect your thoughts, share your perspective, and respectfully disagree with what you saw or with what others say.

###### What if I disagree with something in a Documentary Friday film or discussion?
Say so. Respectful disagreement is welcome and valuable. Take a moment to organize your thoughts before speaking — not to have the perfect answer, but to say something considered rather than reactive. Explain what you think and why, and leave room for someone to push back. Engaging with an argument — not a person — is exactly the kind of discussion these sessions are designed to create.

## Technology and Internet Use

- flow-accordion

###### Can I use the internet however I like during class?
No — and the reason is straightforward: there is not enough time to waste any of it. Internet access is for course-related work only. Video media is restricted to the course files directory (`F:/Class_Files/VIDEO/dd`) unless your coursework or instructor directs you somewhere specific. Social media, messaging apps, entertainment, and anything outside your current coursework are not allowed.

There is one exception: if you are **ahead of your weekly progress expectations**, you may use the last 20 minutes of class to access whatever video or internet content you have available. Stay on pace and that time is yours.

If your instructor sees off-task internet or media use, you will receive a verbal warning. A second offense means you will be directed to return to your housing unit for that session.

###### Can I use AI tools like ChatGPT for assignments?
Talk to your instructor. AI tools are increasingly part of professional design and development workflows and this program takes them seriously as real tools. How and when they are appropriate for specific assignments is something your instructor will address in the relevant courses. Using AI to complete assigned work you are supposed to do yourself is an academic integrity issue regardless of the tool.

## Grading and Academic Integrity

- flow-accordion

###### How does grading work?
Each credit module ends with a graded assessment. Credits 1, 2, and 4 end with a 60-point quiz (45 minutes). Credit 3 is a midterm exam (150 points, 90 minutes). Credit 5 is a final exam (150 points, 120 minutes). Every credit also ends with a major project assignment worth 100 points, graded with a rubric that tells you exactly what is expected before you start.

## Careers and Job Outlook

- flow-accordion

###### Do I need to move to Seattle to find work in this field?
Not necessarily. Web development is one of the most remote-friendly fields in the economy — strong developers in Eastern Washington regularly access Seattle-level salaries without relocating. Graphic design is more location-dependent but increasingly remote-friendly for designers with strong digital portfolios. Spokane is the closest regional tech market and has growing demand. The skills this program builds travel well.

###### What about AI — will these jobs still exist when I finish the program?
Yes, though the nature of the work is shifting. Web development is projected to grow 7% from 2024 to 2034 — classified as much faster than average. Graphic design growth is slower at 2%, and AI tools are genuinely disrupting routine production work in that field. What AI is not replacing is judgment: brand strategy, user research, architectural decisions, creative problem-solving. This program deliberately builds the skills that are hardest to automate, and teaches AI tools as part of a professional workflow rather than treating them as a threat.

###### What jobs can I get with just a short certificate?
A short certificate demonstrates real, specific skills and is a legitimate credential. Entry-level roles within reach include production designer, print shop operator, social media content creator, and freelance logo and layout work (Graphic Design Essentials) — or entry-level web content editor, website assistant, and basic freelance front-end work (Web Design Fundamentals). Continuing into a one-year certificate significantly expands your options and earning potential.

###### What jobs does the full program prepare me for?
The one-year certificates and AAS degree prepare you for roles including graphic designer, marketing designer, branding specialist, front-end web developer, full-stack developer, UX/UI designer, web application developer, creative technologist, and freelance designer or developer. Washington State has one of the highest concentrations of web development and digital design jobs in the country, and the program is specifically built to prepare you for that market.
