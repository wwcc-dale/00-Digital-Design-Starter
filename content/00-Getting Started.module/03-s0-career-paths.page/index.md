# Careers in Digital Design: Who Does What

The digital design field has several distinct roles. They overlap, they borrow from each other, and the boundaries shift over time — especially as AI tools change how work gets done. But each role has a core identity worth understanding before you choose a direction.

### Graphic Designer

A graphic designer solves visual communication problems. A business needs a logo — the graphic designer figures out what it should look like and why. A nonprofit needs a fundraising brochure — the designer decides what goes where, what the hierarchy of information is, which colors feel right, which typeface carries the right tone.

The work is not decorating. It is making decisions about how visual information is organized so that it communicates clearly and effectively to a specific audience.

#### What graphic designers make

- Logos and brand identity systems
- Advertising materials — print ads, billboards, social media graphics
- Packaging design
- Publication layouts — books, magazines, annual reports, brochures
- Marketing collateral — flyers, posters, presentations
- Environmental graphics — signage, wayfinding, displays

#### What skills they use

- Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop (the industry standards)
- Typography — selecting and combining typefaces effectively
- Color theory — using color to communicate mood, hierarchy, and brand
- Layout and composition — deciding where things go and why
- Client communication — translating a client's vague idea into a visual solution

#### Where they work
Design agencies, marketing departments, print shops, in-house brand teams, and as freelancers serving clients directly.

#### The Honest Part (AI Impacts)
AI tools are automating some of the routine production tasks — background removal, image resizing, simple layout variations. What AI cannot do is make the judgment calls: understanding a client's brand, reading a room, knowing when a design solution is genuinely right versus just competent. Designers who embrace AI as a production tool while sharpening their strategic and conceptual skills are the ones best positioned for the next decade.

### UX Designer

A UX (user experience) designer's job is to make sure that digital products — websites, apps, software — are easy and intuitive to use. Where a graphic designer asks "does this look right?", a UX designer asks "does this work the way people expect it to?"

UX is fundamentally research-driven. You talk to actual users. You watch them struggle with confusing interfaces. You study their mental models — the assumptions they bring to a new digital tool. Then you design solutions based on what you learned, test those solutions with real people, and improve based on what you see.

#### What UX designers make

- User research reports — findings from interviews and usability testing
- User personas — composite descriptions of the types of people who use a product
- Journey maps — visual diagrams of the steps a user takes to accomplish a goal
- Wireframes — rough structural blueprints of screens and flows, without visual polish
- Prototypes — interactive mockups for testing before anything is built
- Design specifications — documentation handed off to developers

#### What skills they use

- Research methods — interviews, surveys, usability testing, analytics review
- Figma (the dominant tool for wireframing and prototyping)*
- Information architecture — organizing content so people can find what they need
- Interaction design — defining how a system responds to user actions
- Collaboration — working closely with developers and product managers

#### Where they work
Tech companies, software agencies, government digital services, healthcare technology, financial services — anywhere with a digital product and real users.

#### The Honest Part (AI Impacts)
UX is the most resilient part of the design field right now. The WEF's 2025 Future of Jobs Report identifies UI/UX design as the **8th fastest-growing job category** through 2030. AI can generate interface variations, but it cannot conduct the human judgment required to interpret research, challenge assumptions, and advocate for users inside an organization. That advocacy — knowing when a technically feasible design is still a bad user experience — is the irreplaceable part of the job.

### Front-End Web Developer

A front-end web developer takes designs — wireframes, mockups, visual comps — and builds them into real, working web pages that run in a browser. They write the code that users actually interact with.

Think of it this way: a graphic designer or UX designer produces a picture of what a website should look like. The front-end developer makes the picture real — and makes it respond to clicks, scroll correctly, adapt to different screen sizes, load quickly, and work for people using screen readers.

#### What front-end developers build

- Web pages — structured with HTML
- Visual styling — layout, color, typography, animation with CSS
- Interactivity — menus, forms, dynamic content updates with JavaScript
- Responsive behavior — the same site working well on phones, tablets, and desktops
- Accessibility — ensuring the site works for users with disabilities

#### What skills they use

- HTML — the structure of every web page
- CSS — how pages look (color, layout, spacing, animation)
- JavaScript — how pages behave (interactivity, dynamic content)
- Browser developer tools — debugging what's going wrong
- Version control with Git — tracking changes and collaborating with teammates

#### Where they work
Agencies, tech companies, in-house development teams, and as freelancers building sites for clients.

#### The Honest Part (AI Impacts)
Front-end-only roles are under more pressure than full-stack roles right now. AI tools are good at generating HTML and CSS from a design file — and that is exactly what junior front-end developers spent most of their time doing. Developers who move toward full-stack skills — understanding how data flows, how servers work, how the front end connects to the back end — are significantly more valuable than those who only know the visual layer.

### Full-Stack Web Developer

A full-stack web developer works across the entire application — what the user sees (front end) and what happens behind the scenes (back end). They understand both sides of how a web application is built.

The back end is everything that happens on the server: storing user data in a database, processing form submissions, serving the right content to the right user, handling logins and authentication, talking to external services. Users never see any of it — but without it, modern web applications do not work.

#### What full-stack developers build

- Complete web applications — front end and back end together
- Database-driven sites — where content is stored and retrieved dynamically
- APIs — the interfaces that let different systems talk to each other
- User authentication systems — login, logout, permissions
- Server-side logic — the rules that determine what happens when a user takes an action

#### What skills they use

- Everything a front-end developer knows — HTML, CSS, JavaScript
- A server-side language — commonly Node.js (JavaScript on the server), Python, or PHP
- Databases — SQL for structured data, sometimes NoSQL for flexible data
- APIs — both building them and consuming data from external ones
- Deployment — getting a working application onto a live server where people can use it
- Version control and collaboration tools — Git, GitHub

#### Where they work
Startups (full-stack developers can build entire products with small teams), mid-size tech companies, agencies, and as freelancers building complex client applications.

#### The Honest Part (AI Impacts)
Full-stack development is the most durable specialization in web development right now. The breadth of skill means AI tools are harder to deploy as a direct replacement — there are too many judgment calls across too many different problem domains. The 2025 projections put full-stack demand as stronger than any narrower specialization, and Washington State's job concentration for web and digital interface designers is the highest in the country. This is a good place to build this skill set.

### The Mythical Beast

Some people — not many, but some — develop genuine fluency across all of these disciplines. They can think strategically about a brand. They can conduct user research and interpret what they find. They can wireframe an interface and then build it. They can set up a database, write an API, and deploy the whole thing.

The industry has several names for this person. **Unicorn** is the most common. Some say **T-shaped designer** (deep in one area, broad across others) or **π-shaped** (two deep areas, broad across the rest). In agency culture they sometimes get called a **creative technologist**. In startup culture they are often just called the founding team.

#### What makes them rare
Most skills get deeper the longer you practice them. Design judgment improves with years of looking at and making things. Programming intuition improves with years of debugging and building. Developing genuine depth in both simultaneously is hard — most people naturally gravitate toward one side.

#### Why they are valuable
Organizations lose enormous amounts of time and money to the gap between designers and developers — designers who do not understand technical constraints, developers who do not understand design intent. A person who genuinely bridges that gap can make a team dramatically more effective.
