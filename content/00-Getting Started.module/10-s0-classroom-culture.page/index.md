# Classroom Culture

The Digital Design lab runs differently from most classrooms you have been in.
Understanding the culture before your first session makes the whole experience
go more smoothly.

---

## A Community of Learners

The goal in this room is simple: everyone moves forward.

You are not competing with anyone else. The student sitting next to you might
be three courses ahead of you or three courses behind. That is normal and it
does not affect you. Your job is to make steady progress on your own work,
ask for help when you need it, and offer help when you can.

Over time, students in this program often find that the room has a rhythm to
it — people working hard, occasionally helping each other, asking the
instructor questions, and getting things done. That culture is worth protecting.

---

## No Lectures — How the Room Actually Works

In a traditional class, the instructor talks and students listen. That is not
how this works.

In this room:

- **You work through course materials at your computer.** The materials are
  written to be clear and complete. They include explanations, examples,
  step-by-step instructions, and short videos.

- **Your instructor moves around the room.** They are not at the front.
  They circulate, check in, answer questions, and help students who are stuck.
  If you need them, catch their eye or raise your hand.

- **You do not need to wait for anyone to "cover" the material.** You start
  each item when you are ready and work through it at your own speed — within
  the expected weekly pace.

---

## The Non-Cohort Classroom

Almost every class in this program is **non-cohort**. That means:

- Students enroll at different points in the quarter or year.
- Two students who are in the same room on the same day may be working on
  completely different courses.
- There is no class-wide schedule that says "today everyone is on Chapter 4."

What this means for you: do not look around and worry about where other people
are. Focus on your own module and your own pace. The fact that someone else
is further along than you or further behind you is completely irrelevant.

---

## Rolling Enrollment

New students may join the room throughout the quarter. When a new student
starts, they begin at the beginning of their first course — they do not jump
in at wherever the rest of the room happens to be.

If you were already in the room when a new student arrives, nothing changes
for you. You keep working where you are.

---

## Asking for Help

Ask questions early. Do not sit stuck for 20 minutes hoping the confusion
goes away.

Good questions include:

- "I followed the steps but my screen doesn't look like the example — can
  you look at it?"
- "I don't understand what this instruction is asking me to do."
- "I finished the exercise but I'm not sure if I did it right."
- "I understand what I need to do but I don't know where to find the tool."

There are no bad questions in this room. The instructor has heard every
question before, and answering your question is literally what they are
there to do.

---

## Helping Other Students

When you help a classmate, you learn too. Explaining a concept forces you
to organize your understanding of it. Students who help others often do
better on assessments than students who only focus on their own work.

**The rule:** Help by explaining. Do not do the work for the other person.
Walk them through the thinking. Point to the instruction they need. Ask
them questions that lead them to the answer. That is collaboration.

Completing an assignment for another student — even if they asked you to —
is not allowed.

---

## Respecting the Room

A few things that keep the room working well for everyone:

- **Keep noise at a reasonable level.** Students are working independently
  and do not need distractions.
- **If you use headphones, keep the volume low enough that you can hear
  your name.** Your instructor may call on you or want to check your work.
- **Leave your station clean before you leave.** This is a shared space.
- **If you find a problem with a lab computer,** let your instructor know
  before you leave.

---

## What This Room Is Preparing You For

Every professional design and development environment has its own culture.
But most of them share certain things: people working independently, asking
for help when needed, meeting deadlines, staying focused, and treating
colleagues with respect.

The habits you build in this room are the same ones employers are looking for.
You are not just learning software — you are practicing how to work.
