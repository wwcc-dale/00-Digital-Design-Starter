[lead]The Digital Design lab runs differently from most classrooms you've been in. Before your first session, it's worth understanding how the room works — because the culture here is built around helping everyone move forward, not competing.

# Classroom Culture

## A Community of Learners

The goal in this room is simple: everyone moves forward.

You are not competing with anyone else. The student sitting next to you might
be three courses ahead of you or three courses behind. That is normal and it
does not affect you. Your job is to make steady progress on your own work,
ask for help when you need it, and offer help when you can.

Over time, students in this program often find that the room has a rhythm to
it — people working hard, occasionally helping each other, asking the
instructor questions, and getting things done. That culture is worth protecting.

---

## No Lectures — How the Room Actually Works

In a traditional class, the instructor talks and students listen. That is not
how this works.

In this room:

- **You work through course materials at your computer.** The materials are
  written to be clear and complete. They include explanations, examples,
  step-by-step instructions, and short videos.

- **Your instructor moves around the room.** They are not at the front.
  They circulate, check in, answer questions, and help students who are stuck.
  If you need them, catch their eye or raise your hand.

- **You do not need to wait for anyone to "cover" the material.** Every student is working independently through the curriculum. You start each item when you reach it — the expectation is one credit completed per week, regardless of where you are in the course sequence.

---

## Rolling Enrollment
New students may join the room at any time throughout the quarter. When a new student
starts, they will probably start work on *Intro to Computers & Apps* [CS 110](#pill:info) unless they previously started in Digital Design or are working on business electives. 

If you were already in the room when a new student arrives, nothing changes
for you. You keep working where you are.

- Students enroll at different points in the quarter or year.
- Two students who are in the same room on the same day may be working on
  completely different courses.
- There is no class-wide schedule that says "today everyone is on Chapter 4."

What this means for you: do not look around and worry about where other people
are. Focus on your own module. The fact that someone else
is further along than you or further behind you is completely irrelevant.

## Asking for Help

Ask questions early. Do not sit stuck for 20 minutes hoping the confusion
goes away.

Good questions include:

- "I followed the steps but my screen doesn't look like the example — can
  you look at it?"
- "I don't understand what this instruction is asking me to do."
- "I finished the exercise but I'm not sure if I did it right."
- "I understand what I need to do but I don't know where to find the tool."

There are no bad questions in this room. The instructor has heard every
question before, and answering your question is literally what they are
there to do.

---

## Helping Other Students

When you help a classmate, you learn too. Explaining a concept forces you
to organize your understanding of it. Students who help others often do
better on assessments than students who don't, because they're reinforcing their learning.

**The rule:** Help by explaining. Do not do the work for the other person.
Walk them through the thinking. Point to the instruction they need. Ask
them questions that lead them to the answer. That is collaboration.

Completing an assignment for another student — even if they asked you to —
is not allowed.

## Respecting the Room

A few things that keep the room working well for everyone:

- checklist: Room responsibilities
- Keep noise at a reasonable level — students are working independently
- If using headphones, keep volume low enough that you can hear your name
- Leave your station clean before you leave
- Report any lab computer problems to your instructor before you leave

## Documentary Thursday

Every Thursday, the last hour of class belongs to everyone.

The computers go off. The whole room watches a documentary together — a deliberately different kind of learning. The films are chosen to broaden your frame of reference: what professionals at the top of their field actually accomplish, how creative work gets made, where design and technology intersect with culture, history, and human experience.

> alert: info
>
> **Participation is mandatory.** Log off your computer, put your phone away, and give the film your full attention. Documentary Thursday is not a break — it is a different form of learning, and it counts.

#### Why it matters

Good designers and developers are not just technically skilled — they are curious, informed, and culturally aware. The references you accumulate over time — the things you have seen, read, and watched — become part of how you think and what you make. Documentary Thursday is one way this program deliberately builds that.

After the screening there is time for open discussion. You are not required to say anything, or even agree. Not everyone will like every documentary, but you are expected to be present for it. Some of the most useful conversations in this program happen in those twenty minutes.

Discussion works best when people take a moment to collect their thoughts before speaking — not to have the perfect answer, but to say something considered rather than just reactive. If you disagree with something in the film, or with something another student said, that is welcome. Respectful disagreement is how ideas get tested. The expectation is that you engage with the argument, not the person — say what you think, explain why, and leave room for someone else to push back.

**Don't skip it.** A student who misses Documentary Thursday misses something that cannot be replicated by watching on your own later. The shared experience — seeing it at the same time, in the same room, with the same people — is part of the point.

## Your Journal

Every student in this program keeps a journal — a Word document that is yours from the start and grows with you through each course.

It is not a diary. It is a working document with two purposes:

- **Monday planning** — at the start of each week, write down what you accomplished last week and what you plan to finish this week. This keeps you on track and gives you something concrete to look back on.
- **Thursday reflection** — after Documentary Thursday, jot down what the film made you think about. These entries build up into something more valuable than they seem at first.

The journal is primarily for *you* — to stay organized, to notice your own progress, and to capture ideas while they are fresh. It is also submitted at the end of each course for a grading bonus. Setup instructions are in the Organizing and Submitting page at the end of this module.

## What This Room Is Preparing You For

Every professional design and development environment has its own culture.
But most of them share certain things: people working independently, asking
for help when needed, meeting deadlines, staying focused, and treating
colleagues with respect.

The habits you build in this room are the same ones employers are looking for.
You are not just learning software — you are practicing how to work.
